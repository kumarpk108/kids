"Om! Having bowed down to Narayana and Nara, the most exalted male being, and also to the goddess Saraswati, must the word 'Jaya' be uttered." This great epic, composed by the sage Vyasa, contains the essence of all knowledge and wisdom.
King Shantanu, the ruler of the Kuru dynasty, was wandering along the banks of the river Ganga when he beheld a maiden of surpassing beauty. She was the goddess Ganga herself, who had taken human form. So captivated was the king that he immediately sought her hand in marriage.
Bhishma, the son of Shantanu and Ganga, took a terrible vow of celibacy to enable his father to marry Satyavati. This vow, which would forever prevent him from claiming the throne, earned him the name 'Bhishma' - the one who undertakes a terrible oath.
The palace of illusions was constructed by Maya Danava for the Pandavas. This magnificent structure had many wondrous features, including pools that appeared to be solid ground and floors that looked like water. It was in this palace that Draupadi's laughter would echo through the halls.
Dronacharya tested his students' skill with an unusual demand. He asked each of them to bring him a target, but only Arjuna succeeded. When asked what he saw, Arjuna replied, "I see only the bird's head, and nothing else." This perfect concentration pleased Drona greatly.
The dice game at Hastinapura was a turning point in the epic. Yudhishthira, challenged by Shakuni's loaded dice, lost his kingdom, his brothers, and even himself. Then, in a moment that would seal the fate of millions, he placed Draupadi as a stake and lost her too.
In the court of the Kauravas, when Dushasana tried to disrobe Draupadi, she prayed to Krishna for protection. Miraculously, the garment became endless, and Dushasana grew weary from pulling. The assembled kings witnessed this divine intervention in silence.
The Bhagavad Gita begins on the battlefield of Kurukshetra as Arjuna surveys the armies. Seeing his relatives and teachers arrayed for battle, he is overcome with grief and confusion. He turns to Krishna, saying, "My limbs fail and my mouth is parched, my body trembles and my hair stands on end.
Krishna revealed his cosmic form to Arjuna, showing him the universe with its countless beings, gods, and worlds. Arjuna saw time devouring all creation, and the warriors of both armies rushing into the mouths of that divine form like rivers flowing into the ocean.
Bhishma lay on his bed of arrows, waiting for the auspicious moment to depart from the world. He had the boon to choose his time of death, and he used this time to impart wisdom to Yudhishthira about kingship and duty.
